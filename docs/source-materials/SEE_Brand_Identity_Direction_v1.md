**BRAND IDENTITY DIRECTION  ·  v1**

**What SEE looks like.**

The strategic direction a designer or branding agency executes against.

**Author:**  Erin Berman-Levy (Founder)

**Date:**  April 2026

**Status:**  Internal brief — to be handed to a designer or branding agency for execution

**Pairs with:**  SEE Positioning Framework v1  ·  SEE Naming Decision v2

**What this is NOT:**  A brand book. A brand book is built by a designer in 6–8 weeks, with iteration. This is the brief that ensures they execute in the right strategic direction.

# **TL;DR**

*SEE looks like infrastructure, not like an energy company. Visual centre of gravity: the modern infrastructure / fintech pattern — Stripe, Plaid, Linear, Vanta. Restrained near-monochrome palette with a single accent. Sans-serif typography that signals technical clarity rather than corporate seriousness. Wordmark-led identity — the three letters “SEE” treated as the primary mark. Imagery is abstract and structural; energy iconography is forbidden in v1. The whole brand reads as the calm, dependable system that runs underneath an EPC’s business — not as a participant in the energy-company colour war.*

**Four decisions a designer needs from this brief:**

* **Visual category:** Modern infrastructure / fintech (not energy-tech, not industrial enterprise).

* **Palette posture:** Cool, restrained, near-monochrome with one accent. Visibly distinct from Phoenix’s warmth.

* **Typography posture:** Geometric or neo-grotesque sans, contemporary, technical-feeling.

* **Logo posture:** Wordmark-led. Three letters as the primary mark. Symbol secondary if at all.

# **1\. Visual Centre of Gravity**

The single most important strategic question for visual identity is: which existing visual category does SEE belong to? Brands fail at this when they try to invent a new visual category from scratch. They succeed when they pick an existing one and execute it with discipline.

## **1.1  The four candidate categories**

| Category | Examples | Feel | Verdict for SEE |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Modern infrastructure / fintech | Stripe, Plaid, Vanta, Linear, Notion, Mercury | Calm, technical, confident, restrained. Non-industry-specific. Built for serious B2B users. | RIGHT for SEE |
| Energy-tech consumer | Octopus Energy, Wetility, Sunrun (consumer side) | Warm, optimistic, accessible, slightly playful. Built for B2C energy buyers. | Wrong — SEE is B2B infrastructure, not consumer. |
| Industrial / construction enterprise | Procore, ServiceTitan, Bentley, Autodesk | Capable but visually dated. Heavy use of imagery. Corporate seriousness. | Wrong — we look modern, not legacy. |
| Climate-mission startup | Stripe Climate, Patch, CTVC, Watershed | Earnest, mission-led. Often green palette, idealist tone. | Wrong — SEE is operational infrastructure, not advocacy. |

 

## **1.2  Why “modern infrastructure / fintech” is right**

Three reasons:

1. **It matches what SEE actually is.** SEE is the rails EPCs run their business on — a system, not a service. The infrastructure / fintech visual category was built for exactly this kind of product.

2. **It signals seriousness without legacy heaviness.** EPCs are professional users; they want a product that looks competent. But they don’t want a product that feels like SAP. Modern infrastructure brands signal both.

3. **It positions SEE as deliberately neutral.** The Thesis Memo establishes SEE as OEM-neutral, EPC-neutral, brand-neutral. The infrastructure-fintech visual category is the most neutral category available — it deliberately doesn’t look like “an energy company” or “a solar company” or “a construction company.” It looks like infrastructure.

## **1.3  Two close-but-wrong references the designer should ignore**

* **Octopus Energy.** Often cited as the gold standard of “modern energy brand” — but Octopus is a consumer retail brand. Its purple, its playfulness, its illustration style are calibrated for households making purchase decisions. SEE’s buyers are EPC founders making operational decisions; we don’t need to be playful, and being playful would actively damage the bankability narrative.

* **Procore.** Often cited as the closest functional analog — but Procore’s visual brand is dated and feels legacy. SEE has the chance to leapfrog Procore visually, the way Linear leapfrogged Jira.

## **1.4  The single best reference**

| REFERENCE | Vanta is the closest single visual reference for SEE. B2B compliance infrastructure. Three-letter brandable name (Vanta is four). Restrained near-monochrome palette. Confident wordmark. No industry-specific imagery. Calm, technical, serious without being heavy. Look at vanta.com and how Vanta presents itself — that’s the texture SEE is aiming for. Stripe and Linear are secondary references in the same family. |
| :---- | :---- |

# **2\. Distinctness from Phoenix**

This is a non-negotiable design constraint. Phoenix is warm; SEE is cool. The two brands must read as fully unrelated when seen side by side.

## **2.1  Phoenix’s visual identity (for reference)**

* **Primary colour:** Deep Teal (\#39575C) — a warm, slightly green-leaning teal.

* **Secondary palette:** Dusty Blue, Smoke White, Copper accent (\#C97A40), Amber.

* **Typography:** Gilroy (display), Inter (digital).

* **Overall feel:** Warm, energy-company professional, deliberately approachable.

## **2.2  How SEE differs in every dimension**

| Dimension | Phoenix | SEE |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Temperature | Warm (teal \+ copper) | Cool (near-monochrome \+ one accent) |
| Saturation | Mid-saturated | Low-saturated |
| Imagery use | Energy-industry imagery (panels, wind, light) | Abstract structural imagery only — no energy iconography |
| Typography weight | Display \+ body (Gilroy \+ Inter) | Single technical sans throughout |
| Tone | Approachable, energy-company professional | Calm, technical, restrained |
| Brand emotion | Optimism, capability, warmth | Clarity, dependability, neutrality |

 

| RULE | If SEE’s visual identity could plausibly be mistaken for a Phoenix sub-brand, the design has failed regardless of how well-crafted it is. Test every design decision against this rule. |
| :---- | :---- |

# **3\. Colour Palette Direction**

Specific hex codes are the designer’s job. This section locks the strategic direction the palette must follow.

## **3.1  Posture**

**Restrained, cool, near-monochrome with a single deliberate accent.** The palette should feel like it was chosen by someone who is suspicious of colour, not someone who loves it. Restraint is the signal. The opposite of “solar company palette.”

## **3.2  The recommended palette structure**

4. **A core neutral.** Off-black or very dark grey. Not pure black — that reads as cheap or aggressive. Think the warmth-stripped greys you see in Linear or Vanta.

5. **A secondary neutral.** A warm-stripped white or off-white for backgrounds. Slightly cool, never cream.

6. **A mid-grey.** For body text, secondary UI, supporting elements. Sits comfortably between the two neutrals.

7. **ONE accent colour.** Used sparingly. Possible directions: a deep electric blue (signal: technical), a desaturated teal (signal: subdued reference to energy without committing), or a structural copper used at low saturation (signal: SA-rooted but cool). The right answer depends on Phoenix-distinctness — if the accent collides with Phoenix’s teal/copper, pick something else.

8. **Optional: a semantic palette.** For UI states (success / warning / error). These are functional, not brand. Designer’s call.

## **3.3  Specific instructions**

* **No green.** Every renewable energy brand uses green. SEE doesn’t. This is the easiest single way to look not-like-an-energy-company.

* **No yellow / orange / sunshine palette.** Same reason.

* **No teal in the dominant range.** Phoenix territory.

* **No copper at saturated levels.** Phoenix territory.

* **No gradients in the brand’s primary surfaces.** Gradients read as 2018-era startup. Single flat colours read as modern infrastructure.

## **3.4  Strong reference set**

* **Vanta’s palette** — cool whites, near-blacks, a single restrained accent. The closest visual reference SEE has.

* **Linear’s palette** — dark mode native, monochrome with electric purple accents. Different mood but same restraint logic.

* **Mercury’s palette** — fintech minimalism, soft neutrals, single colour accent.

# **4\. Typography Direction**

When imagery is restrained, typography carries more of the brand. Get it right and SEE feels confident on a page with nothing else; get it wrong and the whole identity collapses.

## **4.1  Posture**

**A single technical sans-serif used across all weights and sizes.** Geometric or neo-grotesque, contemporary, optimised for screen. Avoid display-and-body splits (which is what Phoenix uses with Gilroy \+ Inter). One face, multiple weights, used with discipline.

## **4.2  Specific direction**

* **Sans-serif.** Serif fonts read as legal, editorial, or hospitality. Wrong category for SEE.

* **Geometric or neo-grotesque, not humanist.** Humanist sans (Open Sans, Source Sans) reads friendlier and softer; we want technical clarity.

* **Contemporary, not classic.** Helvetica is a defensible choice but reads as 1970s-corporate. Modern alternatives (Inter, Söhne, Aktiv Grotesk, Geist, IBM Plex Sans) read more like 2026 infrastructure.

* **Variable weights used with discipline.** Body weight, semibold for emphasis, bold for headings. No light/thin weights for body text — they read as fashion-magazine, not as infrastructure.

## **4.3  Strong candidates a designer should evaluate**

| Font | Character | Strong reference |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Inter | Open-source, neutral neo-grotesque, optimised for screen | Used by Linear, Notion, Mercury |
| Söhne | Klim Type Foundry, neo-grotesque with personality | Used by Stripe |
| IBM Plex Sans | Open-source, technical character, slightly humanist | Used by IBM, GitHub |
| Geist | Vercel’s open-source variable typeface, contemporary | Used by Vercel, modern AI startups |
| Aktiv Grotesk | Polished neo-grotesque, broad weight range | Used by mature B2B SaaS |

 

## **4.4  What to avoid**

* **Fonts with strong personality.** Pacifico, Lobster, anything decorative. SEE’s personality comes from restraint, not flourish.

* **Multi-family stacks.** Display \+ body \+ accent fonts. One face is enough; multiple faces signal indecision.

* **Stencil, slab, or condensed display fonts.** Wrong category — reads as construction or industrial design, not infrastructure software.

* **Gilroy or Inter — because Phoenix uses them.** Even though Inter is otherwise a strong candidate, the Phoenix-distinctness rule rules it out. SEE picks something else.

# **5\. Logo Direction**

Three letters is a gift to a logo designer. “SEE” can be a wordmark, a symbol, a combination, or something stranger. This section locks the direction without prescribing the design.

## **5.1  Posture**

**Wordmark-led.** The three letters “SEE”, treated typographically with care, become the primary brand mark. A separate symbol is *optional* — not required — and if used must be a quiet companion to the wordmark, not a competing visual element.

## **5.2  Why wordmark-led**

9. **Three-letter wordmarks are durable.** HBO, IBM, BBC, AWS, ANZ — three letters in clean type carry serious brand weight. This works for SEE.

10. **A literal verb makes the wordmark functional.** “SEE” as a wordmark is also the verb “see” — visually unifying the brand and the product’s value proposition (visibility, oversight, clarity).

11. **Symbols often distract.** A 3-letter wordmark is itself the symbol. Adding a separate icon often weakens both elements.

## **5.3  Wordmark directions a designer should explore**

* **Direction A — Pure typographic.** The three letters set in the chosen typeface, possibly with custom letter-spacing or a single subtle modification (e.g. the dot of an “i”-style accent, a unique spacing relationship). Strongest reference: Stripe, Mercury.

* **Direction B — The double-E lockup.** The two E’s in SEE share visual structure — they could mirror, share a stem, or form a quiet pattern. Done well this is memorable; done badly it’s gimmicky.

* **Direction C — The eye/aperture suggestion.** The two O-shapes inside the E’s, or the visual rhythm of S-E-E, can quietly evoke an eye, a lens, or an aperture without being literal. Subtle is the operating word — if the audience consciously sees “eye,” it’s too much.

## **5.4  What to avoid**

* **Literal eye iconography.** Drawing an actual eye is too on-the-nose and reads as surveillance, which is the wrong association.

* **Sun, panel, leaf, or energy iconography.** Forbidden in the logo just as in any other surface.

* **Hexagons.** The default lazy choice for tech / infrastructure logos. SEE does not use hexagons.

* **Mascots, characters, illustrations as the logo.** Wrong category. SEE is infrastructure, not a consumer app.

## **5.5  Lockup variations**

The designer should produce, at minimum:

12. **Primary lockup.** Wordmark in the chosen typeface, the canonical version of SEE.

13. **Mark-only version.** If a separate symbol exists, the symbol alone for tight contexts (favicon, app icon).

14. **Wordmark \+ tagline lockup.** “SEE — the operating system for energy project developers” in lockup form for hero placements.

15. **Reverse / inverse versions.** Light wordmark on dark background, dark wordmark on light background.

16. **Mono / single-colour version.** Black-only and white-only for printing constraints.

# **6\. Imagery & Iconography**

This section is short because the rule is simple.

## **6.1  The rule**

| RULE | SEE does not use energy industry imagery. No solar panels. No wind turbines. No batteries. No transmission towers. No sun rays. No leaves. No lightning bolts. No green planet earth. No silhouettes of workers in hard hats against sunsets. None of it. |
| :---- | :---- |

 

## **6.2  Why**

17. **Every renewable energy company uses these.** They make SEE look like a competitor to its own users.

18. **They commit SEE to a category posture (“we’re a solar company”) it doesn’t want.** SEE is OEM-neutral and tech-neutral by design; literal solar imagery breaks that.

19. **They date the brand.** The visual conventions of “green energy startup, 2015–present” will look like a period piece by 2030\.

## **6.3  What SEE uses instead**

* **Abstract structural imagery.** Lattices, grids, networks, layered planes, isometric architectural forms. The visual signal: “this is the structure underneath your business.”

* **Product UI screenshots.** Real or carefully designed screenshots of SEE’s actual interface — once the product exists. The product is the imagery. This is what Vanta, Linear, Stripe, and Mercury all do.

* **Geometric typographic compositions.** Headline type as a compositional element. Confident, restrained, contemporary.

* **Photography of people — selectively, sparingly.** If real people appear (case studies, team page), photographed in working environments — not staged in front of solar panels. Photography style is documentary, not aspirational.

## **6.4  Iconography style**

* **Line-based, single-weight, geometric.** Not filled, not coloured, not illustrated. Functional.

* **Reference:** Linear’s icon system, Lucide icons, Heroicons — all examples of the right category.

# **7\. How Voice Extends Into Visual**

The Positioning Framework v1 locked three voice traits: direct, technical without being intimidating, confident, not promotional. Each has a visual analogue. Holding the visual to the same standard as the voice is what makes the brand feel coherent across every surface.

## **7.1  Direct → Generous whitespace**

Direct copy doesn’t hedge or pad. Direct visual design doesn’t pad either — it lets elements breathe. Generous whitespace is itself a directness signal. Stripe’s pages are mostly whitespace; the content is what matters and it’s allowed to be the only thing on the page.

## **7.2  Technical without being intimidating → Crisp typographic hierarchy**

Voice that respects the user’s intelligence translates visually to typographic hierarchy that doesn’t shout. No oversized headlines. No drop shadows. No three-colour gradient text. Headings are larger than body text by a respectable but unaggressive margin. The reader is trusted to scan.

## **7.3  Confident, not promotional → No exclamation marks, no dramatic flourish**

Promotional visual design uses urgency cues — starbursts, badges, “NEW\!” ribbons, banners, animation that demands attention. Confident visual design doesn’t. SEE has no exclamation marks in its copy and no equivalent in its visuals: no blinking elements, no “hero” animations that distract from content, no “TRY IT NOW” buttons in saturated red. Calls to action are clear and unaggressive.

# **8\. Application Examples**

Worked descriptions of how the brand should appear on the surfaces it will most commonly meet the world. These aren’t designs — they’re briefs to the designer for what each surface should feel like.

## **8.1  Website hero**

*A near-empty page. Off-white background. The wordmark SEE in the chosen typeface, small, top-left. Centered or left-aligned in the hero: a single short sentence — “the operating system for energy project developers” — in large but not enormous type. A single understated CTA below it: “Request access” or similar. No imagery in the hero except possibly a quiet, restrained background pattern (lattice / grid). The whole page reads as the kind of website Stripe or Linear would have built.*

## **8.2  Pitch deck cover**

*Off-white or near-black background — either, deliberately. Wordmark SEE, larger, centered or top-left. A single line of supporting copy: “Market sizing & revenue model” or “Strategic Thesis Memo” or whatever the deck is. A footnote line: “For \[investor name\] · confidential.” Nothing else. The cover’s job is to feel calm and serious, not to sell the product. The product sells itself in the next 11 slides.*

## **8.3  LinkedIn company page**

*Wordmark SEE as the profile image. Cover image: an abstract structural composition (lattice, layers, geometric pattern) in the brand’s neutral palette — not a stock photo of solar panels. Tagline: “The operating system for energy project developers.” About section: opens with the canonical short positioning statement; uses the brand’s voice rules (no “leverage,” no “seamless,” no “synergies”).*

## **8.4  Email signature**

*Name. Title. SEE wordmark, small. One line of contact. No photo, no banner, no quote, no “Sent from my iPhone,” no animated GIF, no quarterly campaign banner. Restraint is the brand.*

## **8.5  What an EPC sees in the product**

*When an EPC opens SEE for the first time, the interface should feel like it was built by someone who respects them. Generous whitespace. Crisp typographic hierarchy. Restrained colour. Real, useful information presented without decoration. The first impression: “this is a serious tool, designed by someone who knows what they’re doing.” Not: “this is a fun energy app.”*

# **9\. Handoff to a Designer**

This brief alone is not a brand identity. It’s the strategic frame that lets a designer or branding agency execute in the right direction without being told what to make.

## **9.1  What to hand to a designer**

20. **This brief (1.3c).** The strategic frame.

21. **The Positioning Framework (1.3a).** Voice and language conventions, audiences, differentiators.

22. **The Naming Decision (1.3b v2).** The name SEE, the locked tagline, the legal-vs-trading entity decoupling.

23. **The Strategic Thesis Memo (v2).** The strategic context that all of the above flows from.

## **9.2  What to ask the designer to deliver**

* **Wordmark / logo system:** primary lockup, mark-only, lockup-with-tagline, reverse, mono.

* **Colour palette:** primary, neutrals, accent, semantic. Hex codes, accessibility-tested.

* **Typography:** typeface decision, weight scale, hierarchy, usage rules.

* **Pattern / texture system:** the abstract structural imagery, productionised as a reusable visual element.

* **Iconography:** either licensed (Lucide, Heroicons) or custom in the same style.

* **First applications:** website hero, deck template, business card, email signature, social profile assets.

* **Brand book document:** a 20–40 page document the team uses to maintain consistency over time.

## **9.3  How to evaluate what they produce**

Three tests when looking at any design output:

24. **Phoenix-distinctness test.** Could this plausibly be a Phoenix sub-brand? If yes, reject.

25. **Energy-iconography test.** Are there panels, turbines, leaves, sunshine? If yes, reject.

26. **Vanta / Stripe / Linear texture test.** Could this sit alongside vanta.com, stripe.com, linear.app and feel like it belongs in the same category? If yes, you’re in the right zone.

## **9.4  Budget guidance**

* **Independent designer:** R 80k – R 250k for a complete brand identity executed against this brief, depending on seniority.

* **Branding agency:** R 300k – R 800k+ for the same outputs at agency quality. Includes more iteration, strategy work, and polish.

* **DIY / template-based:** Possible for the wordmark and basic system if you have visual taste — but not recommended at the pre-seed stage if any meaningful capital is being raised. The brand is investor-facing.

*Recommendation: at pre-seed, the right move is a strong independent designer (R 100–150k range) rather than an agency or DIY. Get something good enough to take to investors and design partners. Budget for an agency-level rebrand at Series A when the funded business case justifies it.*

# **Closing Note**

*This brief is the strategic frame, not the brand itself. The brand is built by a designer over the next 4–6 weeks executing against this direction. **The most important thing this brief does is prevent a designer from going off in the wrong direction — because most of what a designer might do, on instinct, would be wrong for SEE.***

*If the designer pushes back on any specific call here, listen. They may be right. But the strategic priorities — distinctness from Phoenix, infrastructure not energy-tech, restraint over flourish, no industry iconography — are non-negotiable. Everything else is craft, and craft is what you’re hiring them for.*

*Restraint is the brand. Calm, technical, infrastructure. The system that runs underneath.*
